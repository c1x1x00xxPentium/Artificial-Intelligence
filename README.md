</tr>
<tr>

The method would be developed further by&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>&nbsp;in the 17th century.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>~1500</th>
<td><a title="Paracelsus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a>&nbsp;claimed to have created an artificial man out of magnetism, sperm and alchemy.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>~1580</th>
<td>Rabbi&nbsp;<a title="Judah Loew ben Bezalel" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judah_Loew_ben_Bezalel">Judah Loew ben Bezalel</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a title="Prague" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prague">Prague</a>&nbsp;is said to have invented the&nbsp;<a title="Golem" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golem">Golem</a>, a clay man brought to life.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>Early 17th century</th>
<td><a title="Ren&eacute; Descartes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes">Ren&eacute; Descartes</a>&nbsp;proposed that bodies of animals are nothing more than complex machines (but that mental phenomena are of a different "substance").</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1620</th>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Francis Bacon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Francis_Bacon">Sir Francis Bacon</a>&nbsp;developed empirical theory of knowledge and introduced inductive logic in his work&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="The New Organon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_Organon">The New Organon</a>, a play on&nbsp;<a title="Aristotle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s title&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="The Organon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Organon">The Organon</a>.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1623</th>
<td><a title="Wilhelm Schickard" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Schickard">Wilhelm Schickard</a>&nbsp;drew a calculating clock on a letter to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Kepler" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler">Kepler</a>. This will be the first of five unsuccessful attempts at designing a&nbsp;<em>direct entry</em>&nbsp;calculating clock in the 17th century (including the designs of&nbsp;<a title="Tito Livio Burattini" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tito_Livio_Burattini">Tito Burattini</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Samuel Morland" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Morland">Samuel Morland</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Ren&eacute; Grillet de Roven" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Grillet_de_Roven">Ren&eacute; Grillet</a>).</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1641</th>
<td><a title="Thomas Hobbes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Leviathan (Hobbes book)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes_book)">Leviathan</a></em>&nbsp;and presented a mechanical, combinatorial theory of cognition. He wrote "...for reason is nothing but reckoning".</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1642</th>
<td><a title="Blaise Pascal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>&nbsp;invented the&nbsp;<a title="Mechanical calculator" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_calculator">mechanical calculator</a>,&nbsp;the first&nbsp;<a title="Digital data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_data">digital</a>&nbsp;<a title="Pascal's calculator" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_calculator">calculating machine</a>.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1672</th>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>&nbsp;improved the earlier machines, making the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Stepped Reckoner" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepped_Reckoner">Stepped Reckoner</a>&nbsp;to do&nbsp;<a title="Multiplication" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication">multiplication</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Division (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics)">division</a>. He also invented the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Binary numeral system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_numeral_system">binary numeral system</a>&nbsp;and envisioned a universal calculus of reasoning (<a title="Alphabet of human thought" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alphabet_of_human_thought">alphabet of human thought</a>) by which arguments could be decided mechanically.&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz">Leibniz</a>&nbsp;worked on assigning a specific number to each and every object in the world, as a prelude to an algebraic solution to all possible problems.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1726</th>
<td><a title="Jonathan Swift" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Gulliver's Travels" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a></em>, which includes this description of&nbsp;<a title="The Engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Engine">the Engine</a>, a machine on the island of&nbsp;<a title="Laputa" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laputa">Laputa</a>: "a Project for improving speculative Knowledge by practical and mechanical Operations " by using this "Contrivance", "the most ignorant Person at a reasonable Charge, and with a little bodily Labour, may write Books in Philosophy, Poetry, Politicks, Law, Mathematicks, and Theology, with the least Assistance from Genius or study."&nbsp;The machine is a parody of&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Ars Magna (Ramon Llull)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ars_Magna_(Ramon_Llull)">Ars Magna</a></em>, one of the inspirations of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>' mechanism.</td>
</tr>
<tr>

</tr>
<tr>

<td><a title="Mary Shelley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Shelley">Mary Shelley</a>&nbsp;published the story of&nbsp;<em><a title="Frankenstein" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenstein">Frankenstein; or the Modern Prometheus</a></em>, a fictional consideration of the ethics of creating&nbsp;<a title="Sentience" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentience">sentient</a>&nbsp;beings.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th>1822&ndash;1859</th>
<td><a title="Charles Babbage" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Ada Lovelace" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ada_Lovelace">Ada Lovelace</a>&nbsp;worked on&nbsp;<a title="Difference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_engine">programmable mechanical calculating machines</a>.</td>
</tr>

<h2 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Glossary of Artificial Intelligence</h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2><span id="A" class="mw-headline">A</span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a title="Abductive logic programming" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abductive_logic_programming">Abductive logic programming</a>&nbsp;&ndash; Abductive logic programming (ALP) is a high-level knowledge-representation framework that can be used to solve problems declaratively based on abductive reasoning. It extends normal logic programming by allowing some predicates to be incompletely defined, declared as abducible predicates.</li>
<li><a title="Abductive reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abductive_reasoning">Abductive reasoning</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (also called abduction,&nbsp;abductive inference,&nbsp;or retroduction) is a form of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Logical inference" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_inference">logical inference</a>&nbsp;which starts with an observation or set of observations then seeks to find the simplest and most likely explanation. This process, unlike&nbsp;<a title="Deductive reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive_reasoning">deductive reasoning</a>, yields a plausible conclusion but does not&nbsp;<a title="Logical positivism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_positivism">positively verify</a>&nbsp;it.</li>
<li><a title="Abstract data type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_data_type">Abstract data type</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a&nbsp;<a title="Mathematical model" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model">mathematical model</a>&nbsp;for&nbsp;<a title="Data type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_type">data types</a>, where a data type is defined by its behavior (<a title="Semantics (computer science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantics_(computer_science)">semantics</a>) from the point of view of a&nbsp;<em>user</em>&nbsp;of the data, specifically in terms of possible values, possible operations on data of this type, and the behavior of these operations.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Abstraction (software engineering)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction_(software_engineering)">Abstraction</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the process of removing physical, spatial, or temporal details&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Attribute (computing)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attribute_(computing)">attributes</a>&nbsp;in the study of objects or&nbsp;<a title="System" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System">systems</a>&nbsp;in order to more closely attend to other details of interest</li>
<li><a title="Accelerating change" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_change">Accelerating change</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a perceived increase in the rate of&nbsp;<a title="Technological change" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technological_change">technological change</a>&nbsp;throughout history, which may suggest faster and more profound change in the future and may or may not be accompanied by equally profound social and cultural change.</li>
<li><a title="Action language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_language">Action language</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a language for specifying&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="State transition system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_transition_system">state transition systems</a>, and is commonly used to create&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Formal model" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_model">formal models</a>&nbsp;of the effects of actions on the world.&nbsp;Action languages are commonly used in the&nbsp;<a title="Artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Robotics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics">robotics</a>&nbsp;domains, where they describe how actions affect the states of systems over time, and may be used for&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Automated planning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_planning">automated planning</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Action model learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_model_learning">Action model learning</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is an area of machine learning concerned with creation and modification of software agent's knowledge about effects and preconditions of the actions that can be executed within its environment. This knowledge is usually represented in logic-based action description language and used as the input for automated planners.</li>
<li><a title="Action selection" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_selection">Action selection</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a way of characterizing the most basic problem of intelligent systems: what to do next. In artificial intelligence and computational cognitive science, "the action selection problem" is typically associated with intelligent agents and animats&mdash;artificial systems that exhibit complex behaviour in an agent environment.</li>
<li><a title="Activation function" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activation_function">Activation function</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Artificial neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network">artificial neural networks</a>, the activation function of a node defines the output of that node given an input or set of inputs.</li>
<li><a title="Adaptive algorithm" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_algorithm">Adaptive algorithm</a>&nbsp;&ndash; an algorithm that changes its behavior at the time it is run, based on&nbsp;<em>a priori</em>&nbsp;defined reward mechanism or criterion.</li>
<li><a title="Adaptive neuro fuzzy inference system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_neuro_fuzzy_inference_system">Adaptive neuro fuzzy inference system</a>&nbsp;&ndash; or adaptive network-based fuzzy inference system (ANFIS) is a kind of&nbsp;<a title="Artificial neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network">artificial neural network</a>&nbsp;that is based on Takagi&ndash;Sugeno fuzzy&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Inference system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_system">inference system</a>. The technique was developed in the early 1990s.&nbsp;Since it integrates both neural networks and&nbsp;<a title="Fuzzy logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic">fuzzy logic</a>&nbsp;principles, it has potential to capture the benefits of both in a single&nbsp;<a class="extiw" title="wikt:framework" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/framework">framework</a>. Its inference system corresponds to a set of fuzzy&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Conditional (programming)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_(programming)">IF&ndash;THEN rules</a>&nbsp;that have learning capability to approximate nonlinear functions.&nbsp;Hence, ANFIS is considered to be a universal estimator.&nbsp;For using the ANFIS in a more efficient and optimal way, one can use the best parameters obtained by genetic algorithm.</li>
<li><a title="Admissible heuristic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Admissible_heuristic">Admissible heuristic</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science">computer science</a>, specifically in&nbsp;<a title="Algorithm" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm">algorithms</a>&nbsp;related to&nbsp;<a title="Pathfinding" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathfinding">pathfinding</a>, a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Heuristic function" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heuristic_function">heuristic function</a>&nbsp;is said to be admissible if it never overestimates the cost of reaching the goal, i.e. the cost it estimates to reach the goal is not higher than the lowest possible cost from the current point in the path.</li>
<li><a title="Affective computing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affective_computing">Affective computing</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (sometimes called artificial emotional intelligence, or emotion AI)&nbsp;is the study and development of systems and devices that can recognize, interpret, process, and simulate human&nbsp;<a title="Affect (psychology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affect_(psychology)">affects</a>. It is an interdisciplinary field spanning&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science">computer science</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology">psychology</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Cognitive science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_science">cognitive science</a>.</li>

Conflicts between arguments are represented by a&nbsp;<a title="Binary relation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation">binary relation</a>&nbsp;on the set of arguments. <ul>
<li><a title="Backpropagation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backpropagation">Backpropagation</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a method used in&nbsp;<a title="Artificial neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network">artificial neural networks</a>&nbsp;to calculate a&nbsp;<a title="Gradient" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient">gradient</a>&nbsp;that is needed in the calculation of the&nbsp;<a title="Artificial neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network#Components_of_an_artificial_neural_network">weights</a>&nbsp;to be used in the network.&nbsp;Backpropagation is shorthand for "the backward propagation of errors," since an error is computed at the output and distributed backwards throughout the network's layers. It is commonly used to train&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Deep neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_neural_network">deep neural networks</a>,&nbsp;a term referring to neural networks with more than one hidden layer.</li>
<li><a title="Backpropagation through time" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backpropagation_through_time">Backpropagation through time</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (BPTT) is a&nbsp;<a title="Gradient" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient">gradient</a>-based technique for training certain types of&nbsp;<a title="Recurrent neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrent_neural_network">recurrent neural networks</a>. It can be used to train&nbsp;<a title="Recurrent neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrent_neural_network#Elman_networks_and_Jordan_networks">Elman networks</a>. The algorithm was independently derived by numerous researchers.</li>
<li><a title="Backward chaining" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backward_chaining">Backward chaining</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (or backward reasoning) is an&nbsp;<a title="Inference" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference">inference</a>&nbsp;method described colloquially as working backward from the goal. It is used in&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Automated theorem prover" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_theorem_prover">automated theorem provers</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Inference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_engine">inference engines</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Proof assistant" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_assistant">proof assistants</a>, and other&nbsp;<a title="Artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;applications.</li>
<li><a title="Bag-of-words model" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag-of-words_model">Bag-of-words model</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a simplifying representation used in&nbsp;<a title="Natural language processing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_language_processing">natural language processing</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Information retrieval" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_retrieval">information retrieval</a>&nbsp;(IR). In this model, a text (such as a sentence or a document) is represented as the&nbsp;<a title="Multiset" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiset">bag (multiset)</a>&nbsp;of its words, disregarding grammar and even word order but keeping&nbsp;<a title="Multiplicity (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicity_(mathematics)">multiplicity</a>. The bag-of-words model has also been&nbsp;<a title="Bag-of-words model in computer vision" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag-of-words_model_in_computer_vision">used for computer vision</a>.&nbsp;The bag-of-words model is commonly used in methods of&nbsp;<a title="Document classification" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Document_classification">document classification</a>&nbsp;where the (frequency of) occurrence of each word is used as a&nbsp;<a title="Feature (machine learning)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_(machine_learning)">feature</a>&nbsp;for training a&nbsp;<a title="Statistical classification" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_classification">classifier</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Bag-of-words model in computer vision" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag-of-words_model_in_computer_vision">Bag-of-words model in computer vision</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In computer vision, the bag-of-words model (BoW model) can be applied to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Image classification" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_classification">image classification</a>, by treating&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Image feature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_feature">image features</a>&nbsp;as words. In document classification, a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Bag of words" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag_of_words">bag of words</a>&nbsp;is a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Sparse vector" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparse_vector">sparse vector</a>&nbsp;of occurrence counts of words; that is, a sparse&nbsp;<a title="Histogram" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histogram">histogram</a>&nbsp;over the vocabulary. In&nbsp;<a title="Computer vision" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_vision">computer vision</a>, a&nbsp;<em>bag of visual words</em>&nbsp;is a vector of occurrence counts of a vocabulary of local image features.</li>
<li><a title="Batch normalization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batch_normalization">Batch normalization</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a technique for improving the performance and stability of&nbsp;<a title="Artificial neural network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network">artificial neural networks</a>. In its basic version the algorithm performs a kind of neighbourhood search combined with global search, and can be used for both&nbsp;<a title="Combinatorial optimization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_optimization">combinatorial optimization</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Continuous optimization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_optimization">continuous optimization</a>. The only condition for the application of the bees algorithm is that some measure of distance between the solutions is defined. The effectiveness and specific abilities of the bees algorithm have been proven in a number of studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Nasrinpour_&amp;_Massah_&amp;_Teshnehlab_2017_55-0" class="reference"></sup></li>

Their strength comes from their ability to create very complex tasks composed of simple tasks, without worrying how the simple tasks are implemented. BTs present some similarities to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="State machine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_machine">hierarchical state machines</a>&nbsp;with the key difference that the main building block of a behavior is a task rather than a state. <li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Belief-desire-intention software model" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief-desire-intention_software_model">Belief-desire-intention software model</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (BDI), is a software model developed for programming&nbsp;<a title="Intelligent agent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_agent">intelligent agents</a>. A third activity, creating the plans in the first place (planning), is not within the scope of the model, and is left to the system designer and programmer.</li>
<li><a title="Bias&ndash;variance tradeoff" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias%E2%80%93variance_tradeoff">Bias&ndash;variance tradeoff</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics">statistics</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning">machine learning</a>, the bias&ndash;variance tradeoff is the property of a set of predictive models whereby models with a lower&nbsp;<a title="Bias of an estimator" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias_of_an_estimator">bias</a>&nbsp;in&nbsp;<a title="Statistical parameter" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_parameter">parameter</a>&nbsp;<a title="Estimation theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estimation_theory">estimation</a>&nbsp;have a higher&nbsp;<a title="Variance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance">variance</a>&nbsp;of the parameter estimates across&nbsp;<a title="Sample (statistics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_(statistics)">samples</a>, and vice versa.</li>

<li><a title="Boolean satisfiability problem" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_satisfiability_problem">Boolean satisfiability problem</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (sometimes called propositional satisfiability problem and abbreviated SATISFIABILITY or SAT) is the problem of determining if there exists an&nbsp;<a title="Interpretation (logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_(logic)">interpretation</a>&nbsp;that&nbsp;<a title="Satisfiability" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satisfiability">satisfies</a>&nbsp;a given&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Boolean logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_logic">Boolean</a>&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Formula (mathematical logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula_(mathematical_logic)">formula</a>. In other words, it asks whether the variables of a given Boolean formula can be consistently replaced by the values TRUE or FALSE in such a way that the formula&nbsp;<a title="Validity (logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Validity_(logic)">evaluates to TRUE</a>. If this is the case, the formula is called&nbsp;<em>satisfiable</em>. On the other hand, if no such assignment exists, the function expressed by the formula is&nbsp;<a title="Contradiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contradiction#Contradiction_in_formal_logic">FALSE</a>&nbsp;for all possible variable assignments and the formula is&nbsp;<em>unsatisfiable</em>. <li><a title="Fast-and-frugal trees" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast-and-frugal_trees">Fast-and-frugal trees</a>&nbsp;&ndash; a type of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Classification tree" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_tree">classification tree</a>. Fast-and-frugal trees can be used as decision-making tools which operate as lexicographic classifiers, and, if required, associate an action (decision) to each class or category.</li>
<li><a title="Feature extraction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_extraction">Feature extraction</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning">machine learning</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Pattern recognition" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition">pattern recognition</a>&nbsp;and in&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Image processing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_processing">image processing</a>, feature extraction starts from an initial set of measured data and builds derived values (<a title="Feature (machine learning)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_(machine_learning)">features</a>) intended to be informative and non-redundant, facilitating the subsequent learning and generalization steps, and in some cases leading to better human interpretations.</li>
<li><a title="Feature learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_learning">Feature learning</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning">machine learning</a>, feature learning or representation learning&nbsp;is a set of techniques that allows a system to automatically discover the representations needed for feature detection or classification from raw data. This replaces manual&nbsp;<a title="Feature engineering" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_engineering">feature engineering</a>&nbsp;and allows a machine to both learn the features and use them to perform a specific task.</li>
<li><a title="Feature selection" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_selection">Feature selection</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning">machine learning</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics">statistics</a>, feature selection, also known as variable selection, attribute selection or variable subset selection, is the process of selecting a subset of relevant&nbsp;<a title="Feature (machine learning)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_(machine_learning)">features</a>&nbsp;(variables, predictors) for use in model construction.</li>
<li><a title="Federated learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federated_learning">Federated learning</a>&nbsp;&ndash; a type of machine learning that allows for training on multiple devices with decentralized data, thus helping preserve the privacy of individual users and their data.</li>
<li><a title="First-order logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic">First-order logic</a>&nbsp;(also known as first-order predicate calculus and predicate logic) &ndash; a collection of&nbsp;<a title="Formal system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_system">formal systems</a>&nbsp;used in&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Philosophy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy">philosophy</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Linguistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics">linguistics</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science">computer science</a>. First-order logic uses&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Quantification (logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantification_(logic)">quantified variables</a>&nbsp;over non-logical objects and allows the use of sentences that contain variables, so that rather than propositions such as&nbsp;<em>Socrates is a man</em>&nbsp;one can have expressions in the form "there exists X such that X is&nbsp;<a title="Socrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates">Socrates</a>&nbsp;and X is a man" and&nbsp;<em>there exists</em>&nbsp;is a quantifier while&nbsp;<em>X</em>&nbsp;is a variable.&nbsp;This distinguishes it from&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Propositional logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propositional_logic">propositional logic</a>, which does not use quantifiers or relations.</li>
<li><a title="Fluent (artificial intelligence)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluent_(artificial_intelligence)">Fluent</a>&nbsp;&ndash;&nbsp;a condition that can change over time. In&nbsp;<a title="Logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic">logical</a>&nbsp;approaches to reasoning about actions, fluents can be represented in&nbsp;<a title="First-order logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic">first-order logic</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Predicate (logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predicate_(logic)">predicates</a>&nbsp;having an argument that depends on time.</li>
<li><a title="Formal language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_language">Formal language</a>&nbsp;&ndash; a set of&nbsp;<a title="String (computer science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science)">words</a>&nbsp;whose&nbsp;<a title="Symbol (formal)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol_(formal)">letters</a>&nbsp;are taken from an&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Alphabet (computer science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alphabet_(computer_science)">alphabet</a>&nbsp;and are&nbsp;<a title="Well-formedness" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-formedness">well-formed</a>&nbsp;according to a specific set of rules.</li>
<li><a title="Forward chaining" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forward_chaining">Forward chaining</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (or forward reasoning) is one of the two main methods of reasoning when using an&nbsp;<a title="Inference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_engine">inference engine</a>&nbsp;and can be described&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Logically" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logically">logically</a>&nbsp;as repeated application of&nbsp;<em><a title="Modus ponens" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modus_ponens">modus ponens</a></em>. Forward chaining is a popular implementation strategy for&nbsp;<a title="Expert system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert_system">expert systems</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Business rules engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_rules_engine">business</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Production system (computer science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Production_system_(computer_science)">production rule systems</a>. The opposite of forward chaining is&nbsp;<a title="Backward chaining" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backward_chaining">backward chaining</a>. Forward chaining starts with the available&nbsp;<a title="Data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data">data</a>&nbsp;and uses inference rules to extract more data (from an end user, for example) until a&nbsp;<a title="Goal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goal">goal</a>&nbsp;is reached. An&nbsp;<a title="Inference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_engine">inference engine</a>&nbsp;using forward chaining searches the inference rules until it finds one where the&nbsp;<a title="Antecedent (logic)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antecedent_(logic)">antecedent</a>&nbsp;(If clause) is known to be true. When such a rule is found, the engine can conclude, or infer, the&nbsp;<a title="Consequent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequent">consequent</a>&nbsp;(Then clause), resulting in the addition of new&nbsp;<a title="Information" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information">information</a>&nbsp;to its data.</li>
<li><a title="Frame (artificial intelligence)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence)">Frame</a>&nbsp;&ndash;&nbsp;an artificial intelligence&nbsp;<a title="Data structure" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_structure">data structure</a>&nbsp;used to divide&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge">knowledge</a>&nbsp;into substructures by representing "<a title="Stereotype" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereotype">stereotyped</a>&nbsp;situations." Frames are the primary data structure used in artificial intelligence&nbsp;<a title="Frame language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_language">frame language</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Frame language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_language">Frame language</a>&nbsp;&ndash; a technology used for&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Knowledge representation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation">knowledge representation</a>&nbsp;in artificial intelligence. Frames are stored as&nbsp;<a title="Ontology (information science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontology_(information_science)">ontologies</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a title="Set theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory">sets</a>&nbsp;and subsets of the&nbsp;<a title="Frame (artificial intelligence)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence)">frame concepts</a>. They are similar to class hierarchies in&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Object-oriented languages" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object-oriented_languages">object-oriented languages</a>&nbsp;although their fundamental design goals are different. Frames are focused on explicit and intuitive representation of knowledge whereas objects focus on&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Encapsulation (object-oriented programming)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encapsulation_(object-oriented_programming)">encapsulation</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Information hiding" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_hiding">information hiding</a>. Frames originated in AI research and objects primarily in&nbsp;<a title="Software engineering" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_engineering">software engineering</a>. However, in practice the techniques and capabilities of frame and object-oriented languages overlap significantly.</li>
<li><a title="Frame problem" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_problem">Frame problem</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the problem of finding adequate collections of axioms for a viable description of a robot environment.</li>
<li><a title="Friendly artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendly_artificial_intelligence">Friendly artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;(also friendly AI or FAI) &ndash; a hypothetical&nbsp;<a title="Artificial general intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence">artificial general intelligence</a>&nbsp;(AGI) that would have a positive effect on humanity. It is a part of the&nbsp;<a title="Ethics of artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics_of_artificial_intelligence">ethics of artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;and is closely related to&nbsp;<a title="Machine ethics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_ethics">machine ethics</a>. While machine ethics is concerned with how an artificially intelligent agent should behave, friendly artificial intelligence research is focused on how to practically bring about this behaviour and ensuring it is adequately constrained.</li>
<li><a title="Futures studies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futures_studies">Futures studies</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the study of postulating possible, probable, and preferable&nbsp;<a title="Future" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future">futures</a>&nbsp;and the worldviews and myths that underlie them.</li>
<li><a title="Fuzzy control system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_control_system">Fuzzy control system</a>&nbsp;&ndash;&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a title="Control system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_system">control system</a>&nbsp;based on&nbsp;<a title="Fuzzy logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic">fuzzy logic</a>&mdash;a&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematical</a>&nbsp;system that analyzes&nbsp;<a title="Analog signal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analog_signal">analog</a>&nbsp;input values in terms of&nbsp;<a title="Mathematical logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic">logical</a>&nbsp;variables that take on continuous values between 0 and 1, in contrast to classical or&nbsp;<a title="Digital data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_data">digital</a>&nbsp;logic, which operates on discrete values of either 1 or 0 (true or false, respectively).</li>
<li><a title="Fuzzy logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic">Fuzzy logic</a>&nbsp;&ndash;&nbsp;a simple form for the&nbsp;<a title="Many-valued logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Many-valued_logic">many-valued logic</a>, in which the&nbsp;<a title="Truth value" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value">truth values</a>&nbsp;of variables may have any degree of "<em>Truthfulness</em>" that can be represented by any real number in the range between 0 (as in Completely False) and 1 (as in Completely True) inclusive. Consequently, It is employed to handle the concept of partial truth, where the truth value may range between completely true and completely false. In contrast to&nbsp;<a title="Boolean algebra" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra">Boolean logic</a>, where the truth values of variables may have the integer values 0 or 1 only.</li>
<li><a title="Fuzzy rule" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_rule">Fuzzy rule</a>&nbsp;&ndash; Fuzzy rules are used within&nbsp;<a title="Fuzzy logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic">fuzzy logic systems</a>&nbsp;to infer an output based on input variables.</li>
<li><a title="Fuzzy set" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_set">Fuzzy set</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In classical&nbsp;<a title="Set theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory">set theory</a>, the membership of elements in a set is assessed in binary terms according to a&nbsp;<a title="Principle of bivalence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_bivalence">bivalent condition</a>&nbsp;&mdash; an element either belongs or does not belong to the set. By contrast, fuzzy set theory permits the gradual assessment of the membership of elements in a set; this is described with the aid of a&nbsp;<a title="Membership function (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membership_function_(mathematics)">membership function</a>&nbsp;valued in the real unit interval [0,&nbsp;1]. Fuzzy sets generalize classical sets, since the&nbsp;<a title="Indicator function" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indicator_function">indicator functions</a>&nbsp;(aka characteristic functions) of classical sets are special cases of the membership functions of fuzzy sets, if the latter only take values 0 or 1.&nbsp;In fuzzy set theory, classical bivalent sets are usually called&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Crisp set" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisp_set"><em>crisp</em>&nbsp;sets</a>. The fuzzy set theory can be used in a wide range of domains in which information is incomplete or imprecise, such as&nbsp;<a title="Bioinformatics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>.</li>
</ul>

<li><a title="Game theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory">Game theory</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the study of&nbsp;<a title="Mathematical model" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model">mathematical models</a>&nbsp;of strategic interaction between rational decision-makers.</li>

<li><a title="Genetic operator" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_operator">Genetic operator</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is an&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Operator (programming)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(programming)">operator</a>&nbsp;used in&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic algorithms" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_algorithms">genetic algorithms</a>&nbsp;to guide the algorithm towards a solution to a given problem. <li><a title="Graph (abstract data type)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(abstract_data_type)">Graph (abstract data type)</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science">computer science</a>, a graph is an&nbsp;<a title="Abstract data type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_data_type">abstract data type</a>&nbsp;that is meant to implement the&nbsp;<a title="Graph (discrete mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)">undirected graph</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Directed graph" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed_graph">directed graph</a>&nbsp;concepts from&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>; specifically, the field of&nbsp;<a title="Graph theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory">graph theory</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Graph (discrete mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)">Graph (discrete mathematics)</a>&nbsp;&ndash; In&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and more specifically in&nbsp;<a title="Graph theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory">graph theory</a>, a graph is a structure amounting to a set of objects in which some pairs of the objects are in some sense "related". The objects correspond to mathematical abstractions called&nbsp;<em><a title="Vertex (graph theory)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(graph_theory)">vertices</a></em>&nbsp;(also called&nbsp;<em>nodes</em>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<em>points</em>) and each of the related pairs of vertices is called an&nbsp;<em>edge</em>&nbsp;(also called an&nbsp;<em>arc</em>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<em>line</em>).</li>

The relationships allow data in the store to be linked together directly, and in many cases retrieved with one operation. Graph databases hold the relationships between data as a priority. Querying relationships within a graph database is fast because they are perpetually stored within the database itself. Relationships can be intuitively visualized using graph databases, making it useful for heavily inter-connected data.</li>

<li><a title="Graph traversal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_traversal">Graph traversal</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (also known as graph search) refers to the process of visiting (checking and/or updating) each vertex in a&nbsp;<a title="Graph (discrete mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)">graph</a>. Typically,&nbsp;<em>information integration</em>&nbsp;refers to textual representations of knowledge but is sometimes applied to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Rich-media" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rich-media">rich-media</a>&nbsp;content. They may be very simple or&nbsp;<a title="Complexity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexity">very complex</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Intelligent control" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_control">Intelligent control</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a class of&nbsp;<a title="Control theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_theory">control</a>&nbsp;techniques that use various&nbsp;<a title="Artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;computing approaches like&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial neural networks" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_networks">neural networks</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Bayesian probability" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayesian_probability">Bayesian probability</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Fuzzy logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic">fuzzy logic</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning">machine learning</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Reinforcement learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinforcement_learning">reinforcement learning</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Evolutionary computation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_computation">evolutionary computation</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Genetic algorithm" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_algorithm">genetic algorithms</a>.</li>

<li><a title="Issue tree" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issue_tree">Issue tree</a>&nbsp;&ndash; Also called logic tree, is a graphical breakdown of a question that dissects it into its different components vertically and that progresses into details as it reads to the right.&nbsp;Issue trees are useful in&nbsp;<a title="Problem solving" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving">problem solving</a>&nbsp;to identify the root causes of a problem as well as to identify its potential solutions. <li><a title="KL-ONE" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KL-ONE">KL-ONE</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is a well known&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Knowledge representation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation">knowledge representation</a>&nbsp;system in the tradition of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Semantic networks" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_networks">semantic networks</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Frame (Artificial intelligence)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(Artificial_intelligence)">frames</a>; that is, it is a&nbsp;<a title="Frame language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_language">frame language</a>. <li><a title="Knowledge acquisition" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_acquisition">Knowledge acquisition</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the process used to define the rules and ontologies required for a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Knowledge-based system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge-based_system">knowledge-based system</a>. <li><a title="Knowledge-based systems" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge-based_systems">Knowledge-based systems</a>&nbsp;&ndash; A knowledge-based system (KBS) is a&nbsp;<a title="Computer program" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_program">computer program</a>&nbsp;that&nbsp;<a title="Automated reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_reasoning">reasons</a>&nbsp;and uses a&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge base" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base">knowledge base</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a title="Problem solving" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving">solve</a>&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Complex systems" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_systems">complex problems</a>. The term is broad and refers to many different kinds of systems. The one common theme that unites all knowledge based systems is an attempt to represent knowledge explicitly and a&nbsp;<a title="Reasoning system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reasoning_system">reasoning system</a>&nbsp;that allows it to derive new knowledge. Thus, a knowledge-based system has two distinguishing features: a&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge base" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base">knowledge base</a>&nbsp;and an&nbsp;<a title="Inference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_engine">inference engine</a>.</li>

<li><a title="Knowledge extraction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_extraction">Knowledge extraction</a>&nbsp;&ndash; is the creation of&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge representation and reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation_and_reasoning">knowledge</a>&nbsp;from structured (<a class="mw-redirect" title="Relational databases" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relational_databases">relational databases</a>,&nbsp;<a title="XML" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XML">XML</a>) and unstructured (<a title="Text (literary theory)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_(literary_theory)">text</a>, documents,&nbsp;<a title="Image" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image">images</a>) sources. The resulting knowledge needs to be in a machine-readable and machine-interpretable format and must&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge representation and reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation_and_reasoning">represent knowledge</a>&nbsp;in a manner that facilitates inferencing. Although it is methodically similar to&nbsp;<a title="Information extraction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_extraction">information extraction</a>&nbsp;(<a title="Natural language processing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_language_processing">NLP</a>) and&nbsp;<a title="Extract, transform, load" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extract,_transform,_load">ETL</a>&nbsp;(data warehouse), the main criteria is that the extraction result goes beyond the creation of structured information or the transformation into a&nbsp;<a title="Database schema" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database_schema">relational schema</a>. It requires either the reuse of existing&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge representation and reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation_and_reasoning">formal knowledge</a>&nbsp;(reusing identifiers or&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ontologies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontologies">ontologies</a>) or the generation of a schema based on the source data.</li>
<li><a title="Knowledge Interchange Format" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_Interchange_Format">Knowledge Interchange Format</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (KIF) is a computer language designed to enable systems to share and re-use information from&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge-based systems" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge-based_systems">knowledge-based systems</a>. PostScript was not designed primarily as a language to store and manipulate documents but rather as an interchange format for systems and devices to share documents. In the same way KIF is meant to facilitate sharing of knowledge across different systems that use different languages, formalisms, platforms, etc.</li>
<li><a title="Knowledge representation and reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation_and_reasoning">Knowledge representation and reasoning</a>&nbsp;&ndash; (KR&sup2;, KR&amp;R) is the field of&nbsp;<a title="Artificial intelligence" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>&nbsp;(AI) dedicated to representing information about the world in a form that a computer system can utilize to solve complex tasks such as&nbsp;<a title="Computer-aided diagnosis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer-aided_diagnosis">diagnosing a medical condition</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Natural language user interface" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_language_user_interface">having a dialog in a natural language</a>. Knowledge representation incorporates findings from psychology about how humans solve problems and represent knowledge in order to design&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Formalism (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formalism_(mathematics)">formalisms</a>&nbsp;that will make complex systems easier to design and build. Knowledge representation and reasoning also incorporates findings from&nbsp;<a title="Logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic">logic</a>&nbsp;to automate various kinds of&nbsp;<em>reasoning</em>, such as the application of rules or the relations of&nbsp;<a title="Set theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory">sets</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Subset" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset">subsets</a>.&nbsp;Examples of knowledge representation formalisms include&nbsp;<a title="Semantic network" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_network">semantic nets</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Systems architecture" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_architecture">systems architecture</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Frame (artificial intelligence)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence)">frames</a>, rules, and&nbsp;<a title="Ontology (information science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontology_(information_science)">ontologies</a>. Examples of&nbsp;<a title="Automated reasoning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_reasoning">automated reasoning</a>&nbsp;engines include&nbsp;<a title="Inference engine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_engine">inference engines</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Automated theorem proving" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_theorem_proving">theorem provers</a>, and classifiers.</li>
